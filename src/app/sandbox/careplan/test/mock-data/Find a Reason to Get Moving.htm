Find a Reason to Get Moving

 

Here are some thoughts and ideas that some people have about physical activity. If you have the same thoughts, the information here may help you find a reason to get moving.

 

“Exercise doesn’t do any good”

It can be easy to think that exercise doesn’t help if you’re only looking at how you feel today. Some benefits of exercise are felt right away, such as lifting your mood, clearing your mind, or helping you sleep better. Other benefits are long-term, such as reducing your risk of heart disease, stroke, high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, and some types of cancer. Regular exercise along with healthy eating may also help you lose weight.

 

If you are an adult, you’ll get the most benefit from exercise if you can do at least 150 minutes a week of moderate-intensity activity in addition to your regular activities. If you take a brisk walk for 10-12 minutes, twice a day that would add up to 150 minutes in no time! Kids should get at least 60 minutes of physical activity every day.

 

You’ll get more benefits if you can exercise at a higher intensity, more often, or for longer periods of time. However, even a little bit of exercise is better than none.

 

It’s also important to do different types of exercise. Any physical activity is good, such as walking, dancing, or playing outside (all ages!) Some types of activity are better for your heart, lungs, bones, and muscles. For example:

·         Aerobic activity: If you’re thinking “leg-warmers” and jumping up and down to music, you’d be right. But you’d also be right if you are thinking about a brisk walk, riding a bike, swimming, playing wheelchair basketball, and many others. Any activity that makes your breathing and heart rate faster are aerobic. Aerobic activity helps your heart and lungs. A good goal for aerobic activity is 150 minutes a week. You can break that into 10 minute walks several days a week and try to work up to about 40 minutes for 3 to 4 days a week. If you can do more intense activities like jogging or running, you could make your goal 75 to 80 minutes a week.

·         Strengthening activity: Yes, working out with weights at a gym is one type of strengthening activity. However, the gym isn’t for everyone. You might find that taking the stairs, carrying a heavy bag, sit-ups, or working with elastic bands are more your style. Strengthening activities help your muscles and bones. Try to do 8 to 10 strengthening exercises on 2 days each week (skip at least one day in between). Repeat each exercise 8 or 12 times, then take a short break before doing the exercise again, or do a different exercise.

·         Flexibility activity: If you’ve ever seen commercials with people stretching or balancing, you’ve seen flexibility activities in motion. Any activity where you use the full range of motion of your joints is helpful. Some examples are stretching, yoga, and Tai chi. You need to be flexible to do other types of activity such as dancing or swimming. Always do stretching exercises after another activity so that your muscles are warmed up. Hold each stretch for 10 to 30 seconds. Repeat 3 or 4 times.

·         Balance activity: If you are an older adult, you may be at risk for falls. Working on your balance may help prevent a fall. Balance activities include backward walking, sideways walking, walking on your toes or heels, and standing from a sitting position. Check with your healthcare provider or a physical therapist to make sure you are doing the balance exercises safely.

 

 

 “I’m not healthy enough to exercise.”

If you’re not used to physical activity, it can be a bit scary to get started, especially if you’re concerned about your health or an injury. It is very important that you talk to your health care provider about the right type and amount of physical activity you need for your health. Your provider may recommend seeing a physical therapist who can teach you to exercise safely.

The truth is, any activity is better than no activity. Experts have found that there are health benefits from physical activity for all people, of all ages, and all abilities. You can start slow, do what you can, and work up to doing more physical activity (more intense activity, longer sessions, or more frequent sessions.)

 

“I hate exercising (the gym smells, it takes too long, I’m too tired, I don’t like to sweat, and so forth).”

It can be hard to find a reason to get moving when so many things get in the way. If you’re not exercising already, you don’t have to go from 0 to 150 overnight. Here are some ideas that others have found helpful:

·         Start slow and work your way up. A 5-minute walk, 2 or 3 times a day can lead to walking 10 to 20 minutes a day several times a week. You can try walking a little faster one day or walking a little further another day. Pace yourself and enjoy the walk!

·         Gyms are great for some people, but walking your dog around the neighborhood, or exercising with soup cans in your bedroom can still provide great benefits. The important thing is to find something you enjoy doing. You can mix things up too. Walking one day, riding a bike, or dancing around your living room are all good activities.

·         Even though it can take some effort to get started, many people find that exercise makes them feel less tired.

·         It’s important to set realistic physical activity goals for yourself. You don’t need to sweat to get the benefits of exercise, but you do need to get your breathing and your heart rate up. Ask yourself if reducing your risk of heart disease or diabetes is worth a few minutes a week. How about preventing a dangerous fall or losing a bit of weight?

 

Make the goal meaningful to you. You (and most of the rest of us!) may never be an Olympic athlete, but you can be an active, healthier you!

 

“It’s not safe to exercise in my neighborhood.”

Being safe is very important. If walking in your neighborhood is not an option, can you think of other places to get some activity? Some people find that exercising in their home, walking around a local mall, or going to a community center is helpful. You might also feel safer if you walk with a buddy. If you do walk in your neighborhood (no matter where you live) it’s important to keep your ID and some information about your health (such as heart disease or diabetes) with you at all times. A cell phone, flashlight, or loud whistle may be helpful also.

 

Bottom line: Safety First.

 

“I don’t like exercising alone”, or “my friends and family won’t go with me.”

Having an exercise buddy is great. You can help your friends and family support you with a little planning. Here’s what some people have found helpful:

·         Let your family and friends know how important this is to you. Even if you’ve had some short-lived efforts in the past, don’t let past efforts discourage you from trying again. There’s an old saying, “fall 7 times, get up 8”!

·         Try to schedule activities that can be fun for the whole family. You could go on a picnic and take a walk together, go skating together or play ball in the yard.

·         Accept whatever support your family and friends are able to give you. If they only walk with you once a week, that’s still better than nothing.

An exercise buddy may not always be possible. Some other things you can do include:

·         Joining a class or group. Many recreation centers have classes or clubs.

·         Use social media to find groups or neighbors who are looking for exercise buddies.

·         Pedometers (step counters), fitness trackers, and phone apps may have ways to keep track of your activity (and have some fun.) You can challenge yourself to take a few more steps every day!
